Project Summary

Thermographic reconstruction and inversion for wind turbine blades to advance renewable energy in Brazil

Project Introduction

Funding, partners, and scope.

This project is funded under the ISPF International Collaboration Awards 2024 (Brazil and South Africa), supporting a UK–Brazil research collaboration led by Northumbria University (UK) and the Federal University of Rio de Janeiro (Brazil). Grant reference: ICAO/R1/241110. Total funding awarded: £208,069.04.

The UK lead applicant is Dr Qiuji Yi (Northumbria University) and the Brazil lead applicant is Professor Cesar Giron Camerini (Federal University of Rio de Janeiro). The project also targets industrial relevance via collaboration and validation pathways with Brazilian stakeholders, including wind turbine blade manufacturing and related curved-geometry assets.

Overview

Motivation, challenges, and project aims (high-level).

Wind energy is a key component of Brazil’s strategy to achieve net-zero carbon emissions by 2050, and wind turbine blades require reliable non-destructive evaluation (NDE) to ensure safe and efficient operation. Thermography is a widely used inspection approach: a controlled thermal stimulus is applied and the surface temperature evolution is monitored to detect subsurface anomalies that alter thermal transport behaviour.

However, conventional thermographic inspection becomes substantially more challenging for wind turbine blades. The blades exhibit complex curved geometries, corners and edges that lead to non-uniform heat distribution and shadowing effects, which can reduce the reliability of defect characterisation. In addition, accurate interpretation requires robust heat-transfer modelling, which is more difficult for complex shapes and composite materials that may exhibit anisotropic thermal behaviour. These issues motivate the need for thermography methods that are explicitly tailored to curved structures and that enable more intuitive, quantitative interpretation.

The overall aim of this project is to improve the effectiveness of wind turbine blade inspections by developing next-generation thermography reconstruction and inversion methods that are better suited to complex geometries. At a high level, the project focuses on: (i) improving heating and data quality for curved composite structures; (ii) developing physics-informed inversion approaches that support depth-aware interpretation (for example, more intuitive internal visualisation rather than purely surface-level contrast); and (iii) validating the methods through industry-relevant inspection scenarios in Brazil, with potential extension to other curved-geometry applications.

Expected Impact

Why the outcomes matter.

The expected impact includes improved defect detection and characterisation capability for composite wind turbine blades, supporting safer operation and more effective maintenance planning. Beyond wind energy, advances in non-destructive testing for complex composite structures can be transferable to other sectors where composite integrity is critical, such as aerospace and other renewable-energy infrastructure.

The project also supports broader sustainability goals by enabling more reliable blade maintenance, extending asset life, and improving wind farm performance. Through UK–Brazil collaboration and engagement with Brazilian partners, the project aims to strengthen applied research capacity and accelerate translation from research outcomes to practical inspection workflows.
