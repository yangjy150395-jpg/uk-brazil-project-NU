Project Summary

Thermographic Reconstruction and Inversion for Wind Turbine Blades to Advance Renewable Energy in Brazil

Overview

Why this project, what we develop, and how it will be validated.

Thermography is an important non-destructive evaluation (NDE) technique for inspecting wind turbine blades. The approach heats the material and monitors surface temperature evolution, where anomalies may indicate subsurface defects due to differences in thermal properties. However, real wind turbine blades present complex curved geometries and heterogeneous composite structures, which can lead to non-uniform heating, shadowing effects, and difficulties in reliable defect characterisation. Traditional thermographic inspection methods often assume simplified heat-transfer conditions. For blades with curved surfaces, edges, and corners, heat flow paths are not straightforward, which increases modelling complexity and may reduce inversion accuracy. These challenges become more pronounced for composite blades with anisotropic thermal behaviour, where accurate forward modelling and robust inversion are essential for quantitative interpretation.

Aims and Objectives

This UK–Brazil collaboration aims to improve the effectiveness of wind turbine blade inspections by developing next-generation thermography reconstruction and inversion methods tailored for complex geometries. The project focuses on: (i) developing new induction coil and flash excitation schemes to improve heating uniformity and sensitivity to subsurface defects in CFRP/GFRP structures; (ii) developing physics-based time inversion approaches to reconstruct thermal data from time to depth, enabling CT-like internal visualisation; and (iii) validating the proposed methods with industrial partners and realistic specimens, with extension to other curved structures such as offshore thermoplastic pipes.

Methodology

The project combines advanced excitation design, physics-based reconstruction, and validation on realistic blade structures. Two key technical directions are emphasised:

WP1 — Curved-surface thermal NDT system using eddy-current and flash thermography

The first work package develops novel excitation configurations to address uneven heating on curved geometries. It includes pulse-compression coded excitations (for improved signal-to-noise ratio and defect resolution), modular inductive coils adaptable to blade curvature, and controlled flash/LED heating arrays to enable uniform surface excitation. The electromagnetic and flash heating units will be integrated to exploit both deeper penetration (induction) and rapid surface heating (flash) for efficient screening.

WP2 — Reconstruction and inversion for induction heating on curved surfaces

The second work package focuses on reconstruction and inversion, leveraging wave-based concepts that transform diffusive thermal fields into wave-like representations. For induction heating, the volumetric nature of heating requires modified transformation kernels and reconstruction strategies, enabling improved internal visualisation and more reliable defect localisation in complex structures.

Novelty and Expected Impact

The novelty lies in tailored experimental configurations for curved blade geometries (including coded excitation and curvature-adaptive coil/array designs), and advanced reconstruction frameworks that account for realistic heat transfer and loss mechanisms. The expected impact includes improved defect detection accuracy and maintenance decision support for wind energy assets, with potential transfer to other industries requiring composite inspection (for example, aerospace structures).

Collaboration and Industrial Validation

The project leverages complementary expertise across the UK and Brazil teams, and aims to validate the proposed approaches through real-world inspection schemes with Brazilian stakeholders, including wind turbine blade manufacturing contexts and curved industrial components.
