Project Summary

Thermographic reconstruction and inversion for wind turbine blades

Project Introduction

Funding, partners, and scope.

This project is funded by the International Science Partnerships Fund (ISPF) under the International Collaboration Awards 2024 – Brazil and South Africa scheme. The awarded grant reference is ICAO/R1/241110, with a total funding value of £208,069.04.

The project is led by Dr Qiuji Yi (Northumbria University, UK) in collaboration with Professor Cesar Giron Camerini (Federal University of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil). It establishes a UK–Brazil research partnership focused on advancing non-destructive testing (NDT) techniques for wind turbine blade inspection, with validation in Brazilian wind-energy contexts.

Overview

Background and technical challenges.

Wind turbine blades are critical structural components whose integrity directly affects energy efficiency and operational safety. Thermography has become an important non-destructive evaluation (NDE) technique for composite structures, where controlled thermal excitation and surface temperature monitoring are used to infer subsurface defects and material degradation [1,2].

In practical applications, wind turbine blades present complex curved geometries, edges, and thickness variations. These features lead to non-uniform heating, lateral heat diffusion, and shadowing effects, which significantly complicate defect characterisation using conventional thermographic approaches [3,4]. The problem is further exacerbated by the anisotropic thermal behaviour of composite materials, which challenges traditional forward modelling and interpretation assumptions [5].

Aims

High-level objectives of the project.

The overall aim of this project is to enhance thermographic inspection capability for wind turbine blades by developing reconstruction and inversion methodologies that are better suited to complex geometries and realistic heat-transfer conditions.

Specifically, the project seeks to: (i) improve the reliability of thermal data acquisition on curved composite surfaces; (ii) enable more intuitive, depth-aware interpretation of thermographic signals through physics-informed inversion; and (iii) demonstrate the applicability of the proposed approaches in industry-relevant inspection scenarios within the Brazilian wind-energy sector [3,6].

Expected Impact

Scientific and industrial relevance.

By improving defect detectability and interpretability for wind turbine blades, the project is expected to support safer operation, more effective maintenance strategies, and extended service life of wind-energy assets. Beyond wind energy, the developed methodologies may be transferable to other composite structures in sectors such as aerospace and renewable-energy infrastructure [4,6].
