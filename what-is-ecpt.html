What is ECPT

Eddy Current Pulsed Thermography (ECPT), also known as induction thermography, is an active infrared thermography technique for non-destructive evaluation of conductive materials and structures.[1][4]

Background

Why ECPT is used in non-destructive testing.

Infrared thermography is widely used in non-destructive testing because it enables non-contact, wide-area inspection and fast visualisation of subsurface anomalies through surface temperature evolution.[1] In active thermography, an external stimulus introduces heat, and a thermal camera records the transient response. Defects and material variations modify heat generation or heat flow, producing measurable thermal contrast on the surface.[1]

ECPT provides the stimulus electromagnetically. A pulsed (or modulated) magnetic field induces eddy currents in an electrically conductive target, producing rapid heating that is strongly linked to electrical and magnetic properties. This coupling is valuable for detecting surface-breaking cracks and near-surface discontinuities that disturb current flow.[2][4]

Physical principle

How electromagnetic heating becomes a thermal image.

In ECPT, an excitation coil generates a time-varying magnetic field. The field induces eddy currents within the skin depth of the specimen, and Joule losses produce a localised temperature rise. For ferromagnetic targets, additional loss mechanisms can contribute to heating depending on the excitation configuration and material state.[2][4]

Defects such as cracks, corrosion features, and inclusions perturb current density and alter heat generation and diffusion pathways. An infrared camera records the transient surface temperature sequence, enabling defect visualisation and quantitative feature extraction (for example, temporal contrast or phase-like metrics under modulated excitation).[2][3][4]

Applications

Typical use cases in engineering inspection.

ECPT and induction thermography are commonly applied to surface crack detection in steel components, rails, gears, and other safety-critical metallic parts. The technique is attractive for automation because it can be non-contact and produces intuitive thermal images that support rapid screening.[3][4]

Beyond crack detection, ECPT has been investigated for characterising corrosion-related damage, material degradation, and other discontinuities where electromagnetic and thermal responses provide complementary sensitivity. Excitation coil design and configuration (for example, line coils, Helmholtz coils, and yoke-based arrangements) strongly influence heating uniformity and inspection coverage.[2][4]

Applications for WTB

How thermography relates to wind turbine blade inspection.

Wind turbine blade (WTB) structures often involve composite materials (GFRP and CFRP), adhesive joints, and layered architectures. Thermography has been studied for detecting delamination-like features and internal structures in WTB-related composites, including outdoor inspection approaches that exploit solar excitation and environmental transients.[5][6]

In laboratory and monitoring contexts, alternative heating strategies (for example, laser-based thermography) have also been explored for WTB inspection, particularly when non-contact operation and large-area coverage are required. These studies motivate modelling and reconstruction methods that account for complex geometry and anisotropic heat transfer in blade structures.[7]
